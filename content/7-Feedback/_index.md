> Below are my sincere feedback and reflections after dedicating 12 weeks to the  First Cloud AI Journey (FCAJ) program. Completing the High-Concurrency Payment Gateway project, the extensive Workshops, and the technical Blogs has left me with invaluable experiences.

### Overall Assessment

**1. Working and Learning Environment**  
The FCAJ community environment is extremely practical and hands-on. Unlike traditional theoretical courses, manually configuring everything from VPCs, EC2s, ALBs to ECS Fargate and RDS made me truly feel the pressure and responsibility of a real Cloud Engineer. The Q&A spaces on Discord/Slack were always active, helping to quickly resolve difficult bugs (like server crashes during k6 load testing).

**2. Support from Mentors / Organizers**  
I was deeply impressed by the mentors' philosophy of "Guiding the way, not doing the work." When I encountered an IAM Policy permission error for the S3 KMS bucket, the mentor did not send the solution JSON file but instead provided documentation and keywords for me to debug it myself. This was frustrating at first but turned out to be the best way to train my Root Cause Analysis mindset.

**3. Alignment with Enterprise Realities**  
The program aligns 100% with the needs of modern enterprises. Knowledge such as deploying Serverless architectures (Lambda, API Gateway), cost optimization (FinOps), and especially high-concurrency systems using Redis (Rate Limiting, Idempotency) are incredibly valuable "weapons" rarely taught in depth at universities. The sheer volume of knowledge completely exceeded my initial expectations.

**4. Opportunities for Learning & Skill Development**  
Besides AWS configuration skills, I also honed many "soft skills for tech folks." This included translating in-depth technical blogs about GraphRAG and NIDS, writing clear budget Proposals, and structuring Workshop documentation so future learners can easily follow. The FCJ Community Day events (Events 1, 2, 3) also strongly inspired me regarding the DevOps career path and how to overcome psychological procrastination.

**5. Culture & Builder Spirit**  
The "Go Build" and "Fail Fast, Learn Faster" mentality was deeply ingrained throughout the 12 weeks. Everyone shared a spirit of not fearing mistakes, ready to tear down and rebuild systems when they crashed. Knowledge sharing was highly valued, proven by the fact that everyone published their Worklogs to learn from one another.

### Additional Questions

- **What were you most satisfied with?**  
The shift in mindset. I no longer write code based on feelings ("vibe code"); instead, I think according to architectural standards, considering security (Private Subnets), performance (Caching), and costs before actually building.
- **What do you think the community needs to improve?**  
  Although the guide is very detailed, configuring CLI or handling permission errors can be a bit challenging for beginners who are new to AWS. The community could add more troubleshooting guides for common issues and provide deeper reference materials to help interns easily overcome initial technical hurdles.
- **Would you recommend this to a friend?**  
100% absolutely. This is not a superficial "walk in the park" program. Anyone who truly wants to endure rigorous training to mature and build an extremely heavy-weight portfolio (Workshop + Proposal) for job hunting must join FCJ.

### Proposals & Desires
- **Suggestions for improvement:** Organize regular short sharing sessions (Showcases or small group discussions). This would be an excellent opportunity for interns to exchange ways to resolve deployment issues, share debugging tips, and learn from each other to improve their skills together.
- **Future direction:** I plan to continue self-study and upgrade my current system to a higher level (such as experimenting with infrastructure automation). At the same time, I will strive to hone my remaining technical skills and accumulate more practical experience to prepare for upcoming career opportunities in the Cloud and DevOps fields.
